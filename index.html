The placement blueprint

Where every muscle goes

Each dot is a real joint position from the model. Across every joint sits an antagonist pair of 0.2 mm nitinol wires (the two coloured lines), offset to either side at the joint's moment arm. Colour = how much work that joint does in the walk, so you know where to spend your best wire and anchoring effort.

CRITICAL — hips (weight-shift & step) HIGH — spine lean MEDIUM — knees, shoulders LIGHT — ankles, elbows
How to read it. The two wires on each joint are the same muscle pair seen from two angles — front shows the roll/side-to-side wires spread apart, side shows the pitch/fore-aft wires spread apart. The green callout on the side view marks the wire-harness exit at the lower back, where all 34 leads gather and run to the driver board. Build to these positions and the gait the policy learned will transfer.
How each muscle mounts

Anchoring one joint

Every joint is built the same way. Two pre-shaped wires, one per side, each crimped to the limb above and the limb below. This is the detail behind every dot in the blueprint.

① Anchor up & down

Crimp each wire to the parent limb ~48% up and the child limb ~48% down. The crimp is both the mechanical anchor and the electrical terminal (nitinol won't solder).

② Far from the pivot

Torque = force × moment arm. Anchor as far from the pivot as the frame allows (~10–13 mm). That's what turns a 5.6 N wire into enough torque to move a hip.

③ One wire per side

The antagonists sit on opposite faces of the joint, kept apart with Kapton/ceramic so they never short. Together they push and pull the joint through its whole range.

Direction matters. Shape-set so knees flex backward and elbows flex forward only — a real Dummy 13 ball-socket can't hyperextend, and neither can the policy's gait. Joint travel targets are in Assembly Step 1.

Assembly

Build it, step by step

1

Print the frame — and lengthen the feet

Print soozafone's Dummy 13 as designed, with one change the sim proved is decisive.

The foot modification (don't skip)

Walking distance is set by foot length, front-to-back — not width. The stock foot (~1.8 cm wide × 1.8 cm long) fails: it tips within a second. Extending to ~2.5 cm long (a small toe + heel) unlocked the full ~2 m walk.

  • Target sole: ~1.8 cm wide × ~2.5 cm long — flat, rigid plates glued/screwed under the stock feet, or edit the foot STL.

2

Shape-set the 34 wires

Out of the bag nitinol remembers being straight. You re-teach each wire its shape by holding it in the target pose and heat-treating — this is what makes the wire be the joint. Per the wiring map, you'll set two shapes per joint: one to the flexed limit, one to the extended limit.

  • Constrain the wire on a steel/ceramic jig in the target shape.
  • ~450–500 °C for ~5–15 min, then water-quench.
  • Label every wire — 34 identical-looking wires is a recipe for confusion.
Safety: 450–500 °C + quenching — ventilate, eye protection, heat gloves, and dial in time/temp on scrap wire first.
3

Mount the muscles to the blueprint

Work joint by joint, following the front/side blueprint for position and the anchor detail for method. For each joint:

  • Crimp the flexed wire on one face, the extended wire on the other, ~48% up the parent limb and ~48% down the child limb.
  • Keep the moment arm as long as the frame allows (~10–13 mm).
  • Run each wire's two leads back toward the lower-back harness point (green callout on the side blueprint).
  • Spend your best wires & anchoring on the red hips and orange spine; the light-blue ankles and elbows can be minimal.
4

Wire the harness & driver board

Land all 34 leads on the driver board in the channel-map order. The XIAO streams duty values over I²C to the PCA9685 expanders; each PCA9685 output drives one low-side MOSFET, one MOSFET per wire:

XIAO ESP32-S3 ──I²C──► PCA9685 ×3 ──PWM──► 34 MOSFET gates

muscle_rail (+) ──[ nitinol wire ]──┬── MOSFET drain
                                    │
PCA9685 PWM ──[100Ω]── gate         source ── GND
                       └──[10kΩ]── GND   # pulldown: off = cold = safe
  • Pick the rail voltage so a full-on segment pulls ≈0.6–1 A (0.2 mm Flexinol is ~29 Ω/m, so short muscles are sub-ohm) — usually a 3.3–5 V buck rail.
  • Tune heat with PWM duty, not by starving current: fast heat, then a low holding duty.

Route the leads — they're part of the mechanism

The wire is the heater, so every muscle's two crimps are also its two terminals: that's 68 conductors running along the limbs back to the lower-back harness. They aren't free — they add mass, and any wire crossing a joint fights that joint's slow muscle. Route them so they cost as little as possible:

  • Cross joints on the neutral axis. Run each lead through/near the pivot so flexing the joint barely changes the lead's length — off-axis routing turns the bundle into a spring the muscle has to overcome.
  • Leave a service loop at every joint. A small slack loop lets the joint reach its full range without the lead going taut or tugging the crimp loose.
  • Bundle proximally, fan out distally. Leads gather as they head inboard, so proximal joints (hips, shoulders) carry the most wire — give those the cleanest, lowest-drag paths.
  • Use fine, flexible lead wire (e.g. 30–34 AWG silicone) and strain-relieve at the torso so motion never pulls on a muscle crimp.
This is in the sim now. The obstacle/climb model adds the harness as ~3.5 g at the lower back plus extra passive damping at each joint (more on the arms, whose leads reach farthest) — so the policy trains against the real, tethered body rather than free-floating muscles.
5

Walk it

Load the trained policy (next section), start it on a test stand with the fan running, then on the floor. Expect to tune the two joints that dominate — hip roll (weight-shift amplitude) and hip pitch (step length) — first.

The gait is a deliberate static one: wide stance → shift weight fully over one foot → lift & swing the other → plant → shift back. Slow is correct; the cadence is capped by how fast the wires cool.

Off-board placement & power

Where the electronics live

The harness point

All 34 leads gather at the lower back / pelvis (the green callout on the side blueprint) and leave as a single thin ribbon. That keeps wire mass centered and low, and gives one clean tether exit.

Off the robot (v1)

The PCA9685 driver board, buck, LiPo and fan sit beside the robot. The ~3 g XIAO can ride on the torso or stay off-board too. The ribbon and a fan hose are the only things attached — get it walking like this before chasing a backpack.

The umbilical (esp. for climbing)

That ribbon + fan hose is a real tether. For flat walking, dress it out the back so it trails behind. For the climbing tasks it matters more: a cord dangling in front will snag the very ledge it's climbing — suspend it from above (a light boom over the rig) so it stays slack and out of the workspace, or move power on-board first.

Two things will bite you:
  • Holding costs power. Unlike a servo, a nitinol joint only resists load while hot — standing still keeps wires warm continuously. Size the battery for the worst-case simultaneous draw (a weight-shift lights up both hips + spine at once), not the average.
  • No airflow, no walking. Without the fan the 0.8 s cool-down stretches past 3 s and the gait stalls. The fan is an actuator, not a comfort feature.
The brain

Run the policy on the XIAO ESP32-S3

The walk is a neural-network policy (PPO) trained in simulation — not hand-coded. It reads the robot's pose and outputs, per joint, how hot to make each wire. It's a tiny MLP, so it runs comfortably on-board the XIAO — no Pi required. Use the dual cores: pin the deterministic 50 Hz control loop to one, leave the other for sensing (and, later, vision).

# CORE 0 — control loop (~50 Hz, deterministic)
while walking:
    obs = read_state()              # IMU + joint feedback + each wire's heat estimate
    act = policy(obs)               # 17 values in [-1,+1] — TFLite-Micro / ESP-NN
    for i, a in enumerate(act):
        duty[flexed_ch[i]]   = clamp(+a)   # +a heats the FLEXED channel
        duty[extended_ch[i]] = clamp(-a)   # -a heats the EXTENDED channel
    i2c_write(pca9685, duty)        # 34 channels over I²C — a few hundred bytes
    sleep(0.02)
  • Fits easily: the policy is ~60→17 with small hidden layers — microseconds on the ESP32-S3 (8 MB PSRAM, vector ops). Run it with TFLite-Micro / ESP-NN, or hand-rolled matmuls.
  • Heat estimate (no sensors): run the same thermal model the sim used — heat fast (τ≈0.15 s), cool slow (τ≈0.8 s) — to estimate each wire's state from its duty history.
  • Pose: the torso IMU + (optional) joint feedback feed read_state(). Start open-loop with the feedforward gait, add feedback if it drifts.
  • Get the model: it lives at mujoco/models/dummy13_sma_far_plates.zip. Export to ONNX → convert to a TFLite-Micro flatbuffer for the XIAO.
The camera is for later — today the walker is blind. The OV2640 on the "Sense" opens a real path to vision (obstacle avoidance, walking toward a target), but the current policy is purely proprioceptive — it never uses an image. Adding sight means training a new vision policy (or a higher-level "where to go" controller that hands setpoints to this walker), running on the second core. Great to have the hardware in place; it just won't improve walking until there's a vision policy to use it.

Reality check

What's proven, and what isn't

✓ Grounded in the sim

  • Joint positions & ranges (from the real STL parts) — the blueprint coordinates.
  • Foot length is decisive — stock fails, ~2.5 cm works (tested across many sizes).
  • The muscle budget — hips/spine dominate, ankles/elbows barely used.
  • The static gait, and that a fan (not faster wire) is the fix for the slow cadence.

⚠ You'll find out on hardware

  • Your exact shape-set recipe (time/temp/quench for your wire & kiln).
  • Real friction & slop in printed ball-sockets vs. the idealized sim.
  • Thermal cross-talk between closely-routed wires.
  • Robustness: even in sim only ~4 of 14 starts walk the full distance — expect to babysit launches and iterate on the hip tuning.
You already have the hard part. The body, the joint limits, the exact muscle placement, the wiring order, the gait, and a trained controller all exist. The build is faithfully reproducing this blueprint in PLA and metal — and the sim has already told you which details actually decide whether it walks.
