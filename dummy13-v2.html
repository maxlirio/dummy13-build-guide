01 Print the frame

The Dummy 13 STLs are in this repo under stl/. Print at 100% scale (the robot stands ≈20 cm). Two additions to the stock frame, both discovered the hard way in training:

  • Back keel — a printed ridge running down the spine (see rear view, marker 2). Without it, a backwards fall settles into a turtle-shell rest the arms are too weak to escape. With it, falls roll to the side or front, which the robot can handle.
  • Front battery bay — the battery must sit on the torso front (front view, marker 2). Rear-mounted, the center of mass lands 12 mm behind the feet and the robot physically cannot stand.

Two purchases people skip, and shouldn't:
The 120° wide-angle lens — the obstacle sensing was trained on a ±60° fan, and the stock ~66° lens physically cannot see it (measured: perception collapses).
The two analog muxes — soldering one wire to each servo's potentiometer wiper and reading it through a mux gives true joint positions. With them the navigation policy scores 83%; with dead-reckoning from commands alone it scores 0%. It is a ~$9, one-evening mod and it is the difference between a robot and a puppet.

04 Mount the electronics

  • XIAO ESP32S3 Sense — chest front, camera lens facing forward and tilted ~15° down. This is the entire brain: it runs the vision net and the walking policy.
  • 2× PCA9685 — stacked on the upper back. Board 1 stays at address 0x40 (legs, channels 0–9); solder jumper A0 on board 2 → 0x41 (arms + spine).
  • MPU-6050 — anywhere rigid on the torso, axes aligned with the body.
  • UBEC — upper back beside the PCA stack; battery leads route over the shoulder.

The pot-tap mod: open each servo, solder one thin wire to the potentiometer's middle pin, close it up, route the wire with the servo lead. 17 wires → two muxes → one ADC pin. Sample all 17 in a fast loop (≥500 Hz) and derive joint velocity by finite difference — the firmware constant for this is already in the exported header.

06 Flash the brain

The trained networks are already exported as portable C in firmware/ — no Python, no frameworks on the robot:

  • dummy13_policy.h / dummy13_infer.c — the navigation policy (82 → 256 → 256 → 17). int8 weights: 91 KB, ~2–3 ms per tick at 240 MHz. Matches the trained model to 10⁻⁶.
  • cam_student.h / cam_infer.c — the camera → obstacle-distance net (96×96 input). ~12 M MACs ≈ 2–3 Hz vision; the 50 Hz control loop reuses the latest estimate between frames.
  • The 50 Hz control loop skeleton, the observation layout, and the servo post-processing (EMA smoothing → slew limit → clip) are documented in firmware/README.md — the sim and the firmware share these constants exactly; do not retune them.

07 Calibrate

  1. Power servos before attaching horns. Command every channel to its stand angle from the table above (MG90S/SG90: 500–2500 µs ≈ ±90°).
  2. Press each horn on so the printed limb sits in the stand pose: knees −12°, ankles +10°, hips +4°, hip roll splayed ±8°, elbows +30°.
  3. Record each pot's ADC reading at −45°, 0°, +45° → per-joint linear map (the pot taps are uncalibrated voltage dividers; three points per joint is enough).

08 First boot — in this order

  1. Stand (zero-action): the stand pose is a stable equilibrium. If it topples, re-check horn alignment and battery placement before anything else.
  2. Walk: flash the pot-hardened walker. Sim-verified: 1.38 m / 12 s, 0 falls / 8 under exactly this sensing.
  3. Recover: crouch it by hand, let go — the get-up policy scores 11/12 under pot sensing.
  4. Navigate: scatter objects, give it a goal. The honest sim numbers under the full hardware condition (camera + pots + int8): 75% crossing success; long multi-goal missions compound per-leg risk (~40% over five commands) — expect that, it is arithmetic, not a fault.
What is verified vs. not: every number above comes from the simulation configured to this exact hardware (masses, servo model, sensing path, quantized weights). What sim cannot promise: servo heat, lens focus, floor texture. Expect a gap; measure it; the training pipeline can absorb what you measure.
